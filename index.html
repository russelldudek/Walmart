Innovation is accelerating. Governance must become an enabler of responsible speed.

Walmart is expanding AI across customer shopping, associate work, operations, and partner ecosystems. The governance challenge is not whether to control AI. It is how to distinguish scalable value from attractive demos - quickly, consistently, and transparently.

03

Encourage learning while stopping one-off pilots from consuming permanent capacity.

04

Automation vs. human authority

Use AI to compress work while keeping accountable people in the decisions that matter.

Seven clauses make the portfolio legible.

Each clause answers a funding or release question. Together, they create a repeatable contract between governance experts, product teams, technology, finance, and operators.

01

Business outcome

Which decision, workflow, risk, or customer obligation improves?

02

Legal basis

What authority, obligation, policy, or permitted use supports the work?

03

Risk and harm

What can go wrong, who could be affected, and what must trigger escalation?

04

Data and security

What data is necessary, protected, retained, logged, or prohibited?

05

Human authority

Which decisions remain human, and where may automation act?

06

Operating ownership

Who owns performance, exceptions, maintenance, adoption, and retirement?

07

Evidence and economics

What proof justifies funding, scaling, holding, or stopping?

Portfolio posture

FUND

Contract is explicit, controls are proportionate, ownership is named, and value evidence is credible.

SHAPE

The opportunity is sound, but one or more clauses need design work before investment or release.

HOLD

Authority, data, ownership, or evaluation gaps create unacceptable ambiguity.

STOP

No defensible legal basis, value case, or controllable risk profile exists.

A governance scorecard should track behavior, not presentation volume.

The measures below are categories for an operating review. Targets require Walmart baselines and leadership agreement.

Portfolio

Decision latency

Elapsed time from intake to Fund, Shape, Hold, or Stop - segmented by risk and complexity.

Value

Realized outcome

Verified efficiency, risk reduction, cost avoidance, quality, or service improvement against an agreed baseline.

Trust

Control evidence

Coverage and quality of evaluation, logging, monitoring, escalation, and human-review evidence.

Adoption

Workflow use

Use by intended roles, exception patterns, workarounds, retraining needs, and sustained behavior change.

Portfolio health

Reuse ratio

How often shared controls, data products, evaluation patterns, and platform capabilities reduce duplicated work.

Learning

Decision quality

How frequently evidence changes a funding, design, release, or retirement decision.

First 120 days: earn the right to standardize.

Begin with tactical empathy and evidence. Do not arrive with a generic governance framework and force the organization to fit it.

Days 1-30

Listen and map

  • Map decision rights, active portfolio, risk tiers, architecture, and executive expectations.
  • Shadow work across Legal, Compliance, Ethics, Investigations, and Security.
  • Baseline intake, cycle time, adoption, spend, and recurring friction.
Days 31-60

Make decisions visible

  • Co-design the minimum Scannable AI Contract.
  • Create a single portfolio view with stage gates and accountable owners.
  • Select two contrasting use cases for evidence-building.
Days 61-90

Run controlled proof

  • Test evaluation, escalation, monitoring, and adoption mechanisms.
  • Establish executive review cadence and finance partnership.
  • Stop, reshape, or fund based on evidence.
Days 91-120

Scale the mechanism

  • Publish reusable patterns, control components, and decision templates.
  • Align talent and capacity to the portfolio roadmap.
  • Set the next two-quarter value and learning agenda.

Can an operations and technology leader succeed inside Global Governance without being a career lawyer?

Yes - when the work is framed correctly. The role does not replace legal, compliance, ethics, investigation, privacy, or security expertise. It builds the operating system through which that expertise can make faster, more consistent, better-instrumented decisions.

My advantage is the ability to work across technical and operational boundaries, make complex work visible, install quality and decision mechanisms, and connect emerging technology to adoption and measurable outcomes. Substantive governance experts retain authority; the technology-operations leader makes that authority scalable.

Questions I would bring to the executive team

Which governance decisions currently take too long - and which delays are prudent rather than waste?

Where does AI demand enter today, and who has the authority to fund, release, constrain, or stop it?

Which legal, privacy, security, and ethics controls are reusable platform capabilities versus one-off reviews?

What evidence would cause the CLO and Governance leadership team to increase investment? What evidence should trigger a stop?

Where has adoption failed even when the technology worked, and what did the organization learn?

How should global standards preserve local legal judgment and accountability?
